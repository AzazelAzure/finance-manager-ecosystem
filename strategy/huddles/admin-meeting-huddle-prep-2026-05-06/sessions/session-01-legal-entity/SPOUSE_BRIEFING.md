## What is this?

Hive Finance Manager (HFM) is a **personal finance app** — a tool that helps people keep track of their money, their bills, and most importantly, answers the question:

> **"How much is actually safe for me to spend before my next payday?"**

It's built specifically for people in the **Philippines** who are living on tight budgets — not for wealthy people optimizing investments. Think of it as a digital replacement for the notebook or mental math that many Filipino families already do to track their cash, bills, and GCash/Maya balances.

The app works on phones and computers through a web browser, and is being built to eventually work even without internet (which matters for areas with spotty connectivity).

---

## Who is it for?

**The main user** is a Filipino household where:

- Income is real but stretched — freelance, part-time, or multi-source
- There are multiple bills competing for the same pesos every month
- GCash or Maya is the primary way money moves
- They've tried other finance apps that didn't understand their reality

**Secondary users** are people with more money who want advanced features (AI-powered predictions, multi-currency tracking). They would pay more and help fund the service for everyone else.

## What needs to happen to start earning?

Before the business can collect any money from customers, several things need to be in place:

1. **A registered business entity** — payment processors (like PayMongo, the PH equivalent of GCash/Maya payment tools for businesses) require a legal business registration before they'll let you collect payments.
2. **A payment system** — the technology to accept payments through GCash and Maya (not credit cards — most of our target users don't have credit cards).
3. **The product needs to be worth paying for** — right now, Pro features are still being built. The free version needs to be useful enough to attract users, and the paid version needs to be clearly better than just using a notebook.
4. **Legal documents** — Terms of Service, Privacy Policy, and Refund Policy need to be written and live on the website.
5. **A way to reach people** — marketing, even if it starts with just a Facebook page and word of mouth.

## Why is your involvement needed?

This is the most important section for this conversation.

### The problem

Philippine law restricts how foreigners can own and operate businesses. As a US citizen living in the Philippines, HitM faces specific legal limitations:

- The **Anti-Dummy Law** (Commonwealth Act No. 108) prohibits arrangements where a foreigner uses a Filipino as a front to operate a business that should be Filipino-owned or -controlled.
- Certain business activities may require Filipino ownership or majority Filipino equity depending on the Foreign Investments Act and the Negative List.
- Payment processors that handle GCash/Maya payments require **Philippine business registration** for merchant accounts (KYB — "Know Your Business" verification).

### The proposed structure

The plan that's been researched (but **not finalized** — that's partly what this huddle is for) is:

1. **You register a PH business** — either a simple sole proprietorship through DTI (Department of Trade and Industry) or a One Person Corporation through SEC. You would be the **registered owner and operator**.
2. **HitM creates a US company (LLC)** — this company would own the software, the code, and the technology.
3. **The two companies have a written contract** — the US company provides the software and technical services to your PH business. Your PH business is the one that faces customers, collects payments, handles PH taxes, and deals with payment processors.

This is a **real** arrangement, not a rubber stamp. The Anti-Dummy law means your involvement must be **genuine**:

### What "genuine involvement" means

You would need to be honestly involved in running the PH side of the business. This doesn't mean coding or technical work — it means:


| Responsibility               | What it looks like in practice                                                                            |
| ---------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Business decisions**       | Having a real say in pricing, marketing direction, and customer-facing policies for PH                    |
| **Financial oversight**      | Access to and awareness of the business bank account, payment processor dashboard, and revenue            |
| **Signing authority**        | Being the person who signs contracts, business registrations, and tax filings (BIR)                       |
| **Customer-facing identity** | The business presents to PH customers under your registered entity, not as a foreign-controlled operation |
| **Compliance awareness**     | Understanding (at a general level) what the business does and how it operates                             |


What it does **NOT** mean:

- ❌ You don't need to code or understand the technology
- ❌ You don't need to work full-time on this
- ❌ You don't need to manage employees day-to-day (though if we hire PH staff, they'd technically be employed by your entity)
- ❌ You don't need to understand every technical decision

### What if this arrangement doesn't work?

This is also something we need to discuss honestly. If for any reason you're not comfortable with this, or if circumstances change, there are **fallback options** — they're just more complicated and potentially more expensive:

- HitM could try to register a business independently once immigration status allows (timeline uncertain — could be 6+ months)
- A different business structure could be explored (but gets more complex)
- The business could operate under a US-only entity serving PH customers (works but creates more tax and compliance burden)

The point is: **your participation is the most practical path**, but it is not the only path, and you should only agree if you're genuinely comfortable with it.

## Questions you might have

**"How much of my time would this take?"**
Day-to-day: very little, especially early on. The main time commitments are: signing registration paperwork, attending one meeting with a lawyer, and reviewing business documents periodically. As the business grows, involvement would grow too — but that's a future discussion.

**"What if the business fails?"**
There's a formal wind-down plan. Users get 90 days notice, refunds are issued, and the software gets released for free. The business closes cleanly. Personal liability depends on the entity type (sole prop = more exposure; corporation = more protection — lawyer topic).

**"Am I personally liable for business debts?"**
Depends on the entity type. With a DTI sole proprietorship: yes, potentially. With an OPC (One Person Corporation): liability is limited to what's invested in the company. This is one reason to discuss entity type carefully.

**"Can we change the arrangement later?"**
Yes. If HitM's immigration status eventually allows direct ownership, the PH entity can be restructured or transferred. The plan accounts for this possibility.

**"What's the worst-case financial exposure?"**
Right now, the business operates on ~₱5,700/month of personal funds. Legal setup could cost ₱50,000–200,000 depending on the path chosen. A first hire would add ₱10,000–35,000/month. All from personal funds until revenue starts.
